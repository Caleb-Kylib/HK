Divorce, Child Custody, and Maintenance under Kenyan Family Law

Understanding your rights and responsibilities in family legal matters

Kenya's family law framework is primarily governed by the Marriage Act, 2014 and the Children Act, 2022, which outline the rights and responsibilities of spouses and parents.

Key Principle

In all matters concerning children, the court's paramount consideration is the best interests of the child, as enshrined in Article 53(2) of the Constitution of Kenya 2010 and the Children Act, 2022.

In divorce proceedings, courts prioritize the welfare of children, ensuring fair custody arrangements and adequate maintenance. The Children Act strengthens child protection measures, emphasizing equal parental responsibility.

Understanding Divorce in Kenya

Divorce in Kenya is governed by the Marriage Act, 2014. The law recognizes both fault-based and no-fault divorce, with the following key aspects:

Grounds for Divorce
  • Adultery
  • Desertion for at least 3 years
  • Irretrievable breakdown of marriage
  • Domestic violence
  • Incurable mental illness
  • Irreconcilable differences
Divorce Process
Filing a petition at the High Court or Kadhi's Court (for Muslim marriages)
Serving the petition to the respondent
Attempt at reconciliation (mandatory 90-day period)
Court proceedings and judgment
Decree nisi (temporary order)
Decree absolute (final divorce order)

Child Custody and Guardianship

The Children Act, 2022, introduced significant changes to child custody laws in Kenya:

Types of Custody
  • Sole Custody: One parent has primary physical custody and makes major decisions.
  • Joint Custody: Both parents share decision-making and physical custody.
  • Split Custody: Siblings are divided between parents (rare and generally discouraged).
  • Bird's Nest Custody: Children remain in the family home while parents rotate in and out.
Factors Considered in Custody Decisions
  • Child's age, sex, and wishes (if of sufficient age)
  • Parents' ability to provide for the child's needs
  • Child's current living situation and school arrangements
  • Mental and physical health of all parties
  • History of domestic violence or substance abuse
  • Child's relationship with each parent and siblings

Child Maintenance and Support

Both parents have a legal obligation to maintain their children. The court considers various factors when determining child support, including:

Factor Description
Parents' Income Earnings, investments, and financial resources of both parents
Child's Needs Education, healthcare, special needs, and standard of living
Custody Arrangement Time spent with each parent and associated costs
Other Dependents Financial responsibilities to other children or family members

Alternative Dispute Resolution in Family Law

Couples are encouraged to pursue mediation before litigation, preserving dignity and reducing emotional strain. A family lawyer can guide clients through custody agreements, property division, and maintenance orders to ensure compliance with statutory requirements.

Mediation

A neutral third party helps couples reach mutually acceptable agreements on divorce terms, custody, and support.

Collaborative Law

Each spouse has their own attorney, but all parties commit to resolving issues without going to court.

Arbitration

A private judge makes binding decisions on disputed issues, offering more control and privacy than court.
