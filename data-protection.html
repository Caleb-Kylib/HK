Data Protection and Privacy Regulations in Kenya

Published on Nov 18, 2025 | By Maiyani & Co Advocates

In an increasingly digital world, the protection of personal data has become a critical concern for individuals, businesses, and governments alike. Kenya has emerged as a leader in Africa with a robust data protection framework, anchored in the Data Protection Act, 2019 (DPA), which gives effect to Article 31 of the Constitution of Kenya, 2010, guaranteeing the right to privacy. As of November 2025, the DPA remains the cornerstone legislation, supported by subsidiary regulations and active enforcement by the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner (ODPC). While a Data Protection (Amendment) Bill, 2025 has been proposed to strengthen enforcement, broaden complaint mechanisms, and address emerging issues like AI and data sharing, it is yet to be enacted.

This article offers a clear overview of Kenya's data protection landscape, key principles, rights and obligations, enforcement mechanisms, and practical compliance steps for organizations.

Key Legislation and Regulatory Framework

  • Data Protection Act, 2019: Primary law regulating processing of personal data.
  • Data Protection (General) Regulations, 2021: Guidance on rights, breach notifications, DPIAs.
  • Data Protection (Civil Registration) Regulations, 2020: Specific to civil registries.
  • Other Sectoral Rules: Health data, complaints handling, ICT framework rules.

The ODPC oversees compliance, handles complaints, issues guidance, and conducts audits and inspections.

Who Does the Law Apply To?

The DPA applies to:

  • Data controllers and processors established in Kenya.
  • Entities outside Kenya processing personal data of Kenyan data subjects.

Personal data includes names, ID numbers, location data, online identifiers, and sensitive categories like health, biometric, or ethnic data.

Core Data Protection Principles

  1. Lawfulness, Fairness, and Transparency
  2. Purpose Limitation
  3. Data Minimisation
  4. Accuracy
  5. Storage Limitation
  6. Integrity and Confidentiality
  7. Accountability
  8. Data Protection by Design and Default

Rights of Data Subjects

  • Right to be informed about data collection
  • Right of access to personal data
  • Right to rectification
  • Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten")
  • Right to restrict or object to processing
  • Right to data portability
  • Rights regarding automated decision-making

Obligations for Organizations

  • Appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO)
  • Register with the ODPC
  • Conduct DPIAs for high-risk processing
  • Notify breaches within 72 hours
  • Follow rules on cross-border transfers
  • Ensure data localization for certain critical systems

Enforcement and Penalties

The ODPC has powers to investigate, audit, issue enforcement notices, and levy fines:

  • Up to KES 5 million or 1% of annual turnover for serious violations
  • Lower fines for minor breaches

Proposed amendments include higher penalties and a Data Protection Appeals Tribunal.

Recent Developments (as of Nov 2025)

  • Data Protection (Amendment) Bill, 2025 – higher penalties, broader complaint rights
  • New compliance audits and enforcement regulations in progress
  • Focus on AI, digital identity, and telecom data handling
  • ODPC 2025–2029 Strategic Plan emphasizes compliance drives

Practical Steps for Compliance

  1. Map data flows and conduct gap analysis
  2. Update privacy policies and notices
  3. Train staff and appoint DPO if required
  4. Implement security measures (encryption, access logs)
  5. Prepare breach response and DPIA templates
  6. Register with the ODPC if applicable

Conclusion

Kenya's data protection regime is mature, GDPR-inspired, and actively enforced, positioning the country as a regional benchmark. Proposed enhancements make compliance essential for organizations.
