Starting a Business in Kenya as a Foreigner 2025: Complete Step-by-Step Guide

How to legally register, capitalise, and operate a foreign-owned business in Kenya (2025)

Published: 24 November 2025   |   Author: Your Firm Name – Corporate & Commercial Law Department

Kenya is consistently ranked among the top 3 easiest places to do business in East Africa (World Bank 2025). In 2025 foreign investors enjoy 100% ownership in almost all sectors, zero minimum share capital for private companies, and fully online registration. This guide gives you the exact steps to incorporate and start trading legally and quickly.

1. Can a Foreigner Own 100% of a Kenyan Company in 2025?

YES — with limited exceptions. The following sectors require Kenyan majority ownership (at least 51%):

  • Telecommunications (outside EPZ)
  • Local transportation
  • Security services
  • Betting & gambling (land-based)
  • Mining of certain minerals requiring artisanal licences

All other sectors (real estate, tech, manufacturing, hospitality, e-commerce, renewable energy, etc.) are open to 100% foreign ownership.

2. Best Business Structure for Foreigners in 2025

Structure Minimum Shareholders Minimum Capital Time to Register Best For
Private Company Limited by Shares 1 (can be 100% foreign) ZERO (removed in 2023) 3–7 days Most common choice
Public Limited Company 2 KES 6.75M 2–4 weeks If planning to list later
Branch of Foreign Company N/A None 2–4 weeks Temporary projects
Limited Liability Partnership 2 None 1 week Professional services firms
EPZ / SEZ Company 1 None 2 weeks Export-oriented businesses

About 95% of foreign investors choose the Private Company Limited by Shares.

3. Step-by-Step Registration Process in 2025 (100% Online)

  1. Name Search & Reservation (eCitizen) — ~1 hour. Cost: KES 150 per name. Reserve 3 names (valid 30 days).
  2. Prepare Documents — We draft these in 24 hours:
       • Memorandum & Articles of Association (new Table A 2024 template)
       • Form CR1 (Beneficial Ownership Register – mandatory)
       • Form CR2 (Registered office)
       • Copies of passport + passport photo (colour)
       • Proof of address (utility bill)
  3. Beneficial Ownership Disclosure — Mandatory: disclose every shareholder with ≥10% ownership (ID/passport, phone, email, address, percentage). Penalties up to KES 500,000 for non-compliance.
  4. Pay & Register (eCitizen / BRS portal) — Registration fee: KES 10,600 (flat for 1–20 shares). Certificate issued within 24–72 hours.
  5. Post-Registration Compliance (within 30 days) — Open corporate bank account (KCB, Equity, Stanbic, Absa accept 100% foreign companies); apply for KRA PIN (company + directors); register for iTax & eTIMS; obtain single business permit from county government.
Typical turnaround: 5–10 working days (government part). Total government cost typically KES 25,000–40,000.

4. Minimum Local Requirements in 2025

  • Registered office in Kenya (virtual office acceptable – ~KES 5,000/month).
  • At least 1 director resident in Kenya OR appoint a resident director (many firms offer resident director services for KES 8,000–15,000/month).
  • Company secretary is no longer mandatory (removed in 2023).

5. Sector-Specific Licences You May Need

  • Tourism: TRA licence
  • Health & education: Ministry approvals
  • Manufacturing: NEMA, KEBS compliance, county fire certificate
  • Fintech & crypto: CMA sandbox or full licence
  • Alcohol & pharmaceuticals: NACADA / Pharmacy Board licences

6. Tax Incentives for Foreign Investors (2025)

  • 10-year tax holiday in SEZs.
  • 15% corporate tax (instead of 30%) for the first 10 years for companies creating ≥500 Kenyan jobs.
  • VAT & customs duty exemption on imported machinery.
  • 100% investment deduction on new factory buildings outside Nairobi/Mombasa.

7. Work Permits for Foreign Directors / Shareholders

If you will be active in the company you will typically need a Class A (Investor) or Class D permit. See our Kenya Immigration Guide 2025 for full permit details and application steps.

8. Common Mistakes Foreigners Make (and How We Prevent Them)

  • Using unregistered “business agents” — only advocates may lawfully register companies and represent you.
  • Failing to file Beneficial Ownership — penalties up to KES 500,000.
  • Opening a personal bank account instead of a corporate account.
  • Starting to trade before obtaining KRA PIN and eTIMS registration — this can trigger fines up to KES 1,000,000.
