Understanding Divorce Laws in Kenya

Published on Nov 18, 2025 | By Maiyani & Co Advocates

Divorce can be one of the most challenging experiences in life, involving emotional, financial, and legal complexities. In Kenya, divorce laws are designed to provide a structured process for dissolving marriages while protecting the rights of spouses and children. As of November 2025, Kenya maintains a primarily fault-based divorce system, meaning that to obtain a divorce, one spouse must prove that the other is at fault for the breakdown of the marriage. Recent attempts to introduce no-fault divorce by mutual consent, including a proposed amendment bill in 2023 and a constitutional challenge, were rejected by the High Court in April 2025, upholding the current framework under the Marriage Act, 2014.

This article provides a comprehensive overview of divorce laws in Kenya, including the types of marriages recognized, grounds for divorce, the legal process, and key issues such as child custody, maintenance, and division of matrimonial property.

Types of Marriages Recognized in Kenya

Under the Marriage Act, 2014, Kenya recognizes five main types of marriages:

  • Christian Marriages – Monogamous unions celebrated in a church or religious setting.
  • Civil Marriages – Monogamous unions registered with the state (e.g., at the Registrar of Marriages).
  • Customary Marriages – Potentially polygamous unions conducted according to the customs of Kenyan communities (must be registered).
  • Hindu Marriages – Monogamous unions following Hindu rites.
  • Islamic Marriages – Potentially polygamous unions governed by Islamic law (dissolution handled by Kadhi's Courts).

All marriages must be registered to be legally valid, and the grounds for divorce generally apply across most types, except Islamic marriages, which follow Sharia principles.

Grounds for Divorce

Kenya does not recognize "no-fault" divorce purely by mutual consent. One spouse (the petitioner) must prove at least one statutory ground for divorce. Common grounds under the Marriage Act, 2014 (applicable to Christian, Civil, Hindu, and Customary marriages) include:

  • Adultery – Voluntary sexual intercourse with a third party.
  • Cruelty – Physical or mental harm that makes cohabitation intolerable.
  • Desertion – Abandonment without cause for at least three years.
  • Irretrievable Breakdown – The marriage has broken down to the point of no reconciliation.
  • Exceptional Depravity – Severe moral misconduct (less commonly invoked).

For customary marriages, additional grounds under customary law may apply. Proof is required on a balance of probabilities.

Note: Civil marriages generally require the union to have lasted at least three years before a petition can be filed, though courts may waive this in exceptional cases.

The Divorce Process in Kenya

  1. Filing a Petition – The petitioner files a divorce petition outlining the grounds and facts, supported by an affidavit.
  2. Service – The petition is served on the respondent (the other spouse).
  3. Response – The respondent may file an answer or choose not to contest (undefended divorce).
  4. Hearing/Mediation – Courts often encourage mediation first. If uncontested or proven, the court issues a Decree Nisi (provisional divorce).
  5. Decree Absolute – Final decree after a waiting period (usually six weeks), officially ending the marriage.

The process can take 6–24 months or longer if contested. Costs vary but include filing fees, lawyer fees, and potential expert witnesses. Parties can represent themselves, but legal representation is strongly recommended.

Child Custody and Maintenance

The Children's Act, 2022 and the Constitution prioritize the best interests of the child. Both parents have equal parental responsibility, regardless of marital status.

  • Custody Types: Joint custody or sole custody based on the child’s best interest.
  • Maintenance: Both parents are responsible for providing for the child’s needs. Courts determine payments based on income, needs, and lifestyle.

Division of Matrimonial Property

The Matrimonial Property Act, 2013 governs property division. Key points:

  • No automatic 50/50 split; division is based on contributions (monetary or non-monetary).
  • Matrimonial property includes family home, household goods, and assets acquired during marriage.
  • Pre-marriage property or gifts/inheritances are generally excluded unless converted to joint use.

Conclusion

Navigating divorce in Kenya requires understanding your rights under the law. Courts encourage amicable resolutions to minimize harm, especially to children.
